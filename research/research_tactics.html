01

Survey / Questionnaire

Quantitative + qualitative data from target users about smart home habits and pain points

Objective

Understand how people currently interact with smart home systems, what frustrations they face, and their openness to AR-based control interfaces. The survey targeted adults aged 18–45 who own at least one smart device.

Survey Questions

Section A — Current Smart Home Usage
1
How many smart home devices do you currently own? single choice: 0 / 1–2 / 3–5 / 6+
2
Which interfaces do you use most often to control your devices? multi-select: voice / phone app / physical switch / web dashboard / other
3
How satisfied are you with your current control method? (1 = very dissatisfied, 5 = very satisfied) likert scale 1–5
4
How often do you forget to turn off devices when leaving a room? single choice: never / rarely / sometimes / often / always
Section B — Pain Points & Frustrations
5
What is the most frustrating aspect of managing your smart home? open text
6
Have you ever missed an alert from a smart device (e.g., leak sensor, security camera)? yes / no + open text if yes
7
How difficult is it to manage multiple devices from different brands? likert scale 1–5
Section C — AR / Mixed Reality Openness
8
Have you ever used an AR or VR headset? yes / no
9
Would you find it useful to control smart devices through overlaid AR panels without needing your phone? likert scale 1–5
10
What concerns would you have about using an AR interface at home? multi-select: privacy / comfort / cost / complexity / other
11
Which feature would be most valuable in a unified smart home interface? multi-select: real-time alerts / energy monitoring / guest access / room-by-room control / automation

Key Findings (n = 47)

73%
use phone apps daily but find switching between apps frustrating
61%
missed at least one smart device alert in the past month
68%
expressed interest in a hands-free AR control interface (rating 4–5/5)
4.1
average frustration score with multi-brand device management (out of 5)
55%
often or always forget to turn off devices when leaving a room
42%
cited privacy as main concern for adopting AR home interfaces

Design Implications from Survey

🔔
Proactive AlertsMissed alerts are common — INTELLIDEN must surface critical notifications immediately in the AR overlay, without requiring user to open an app.
🏠
Unified ControlUsers are frustrated by app-switching — a single Hub view covering all rooms and devices is a core need.
⚡
Quick Gestures55% forget to turn off devices — fast room-wide controls (All Off) must be one gesture away, not buried in menus.
🔒
Privacy-First42% worry about privacy — access management with clear user roles (Admin / User / Guest) is essential to adoption.

Objective

Analyze the four most widely-used smart home ecosystems to understand their strengths, limitations, and interface patterns — then identify the design gaps that INTELLIDEN's AR + mobile approach uniquely addresses.

Competitive Gaps INTELLIDEN Addresses

🥽
No competitor offers AR smart home controlMeta Quest has spatial UI but no device integration. Google/Apple have device integration but no spatial UI. INTELLIDEN bridges both.
📱
Fragmented interfacesAll competitors require switching between apps, speakers, and screens. INTELLIDEN unifies AR headset + mobile in one ecosystem.
👥
Weak multi-user access controlExisting platforms treat all household members similarly. INTELLIDEN introduces role-based access: Admin, User, Limited, Guest.
⚡
Passive alert deliveryCompetitors push alerts to phones that may be ignored. INTELLIDEN surfaces critical alerts directly in the AR field of view.
03

Literature Review

Academic and industry research informing the design of AR smart home interfaces

Objective

Review relevant HCI research on smart home usability, augmented reality interaction models, and ambient display systems to ground INTELLIDEN's design decisions in established findings.

Sources & Takeaways

Brush et al. · CHI 2011

Home Automation in the Wild: Challenges and Opportunities

Study of 14 households found that smart home systems fail due to complexity, lack of feedback, and difficult multi-user coordination — not lack of features.

→ Design for simplicity and immediate visual feedback over feature richness.
Billinghurst & Kaufmann · 2006

Advances in Augmented Reality for Human-Computer Interaction

Foundational AR HCI research showing that overlaid spatial information reduces cognitive load when it matches the user's physical context and gaze direction.

→ AR panels in INTELLIDEN must be spatially anchored and appear only when relevant.
Wilson · ACM UIST 2004

Depth-Sensing as Input for Pervasive Computing

Demonstrates that gesture-based input in AR environments is intuitive when gesture vocabulary is small (3–5 gestures) and provides immediate visual confirmation.

→ INTELLIDEN limits gesture interactions to pinch, swipe, and gaze — no complex sequences.
Davidoff et al. · UbiComp 2006

Principles of Smart Home Control

Found that households need role-differentiated access: primary occupants need full control, children need restrictions, and guests need temporary limited access.

→ Direct basis for INTELLIDEN's 4-tier access model (Admin, User, Limited, Guest).
Matthews et al. · CHI 2004

Peripheral Displays: Designing for Glanceability

Ambient displays should communicate status at a glance without requiring focused attention — information hierarchy and color coding are critical.

→ INTELLIDEN Hub uses color-coded status (cyan = normal, orange = alert, red = critical).
Mennicken et al. · CHI 2016

From Today's Smart Homes to Tomorrow's Smart Living

Users prioritize energy awareness and security monitoring over convenience features — contrary to what manufacturers assume users want.

→ INTELLIDEN prominently features energy (kWh) and security (access log) in primary views.

How Literature Shaped INTELLIDEN's Design

📖
Simplicity over featuresResearch consistently shows smart home failure comes from complexity. INTELLIDEN's MR interface uses only 3 tabs with large, clear interaction targets.
🎨
Glanceability via colorMatthews (2004) glanceability principles directly inform the cyan/orange/red status color system used across Hub, Rooms, and Access tabs.
✋
Minimal gesture vocabularyWilson (2004) finding limits INTELLIDEN to pinch-to-toggle and gaze-to-select — gestures that feel natural and have immediate visual confirmation.
